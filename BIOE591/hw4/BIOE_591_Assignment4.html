Deforestation of mountain slopes has been proposed as a way to provide more runoff water for irrigation in the western United States. The idea is that more snowmelt will flow into reservoirs while less will be transpired away by trees. Is this a sensible idea?

Considering the examples listed from The Water Resources Bulletin, Volume 19, June, 1983, this general principle of deforestation for more runoff water does have empirical evidence: >Water Yield Augmentation from Hardwoods We know from scientific studies begun nearly 50 years ago that interruption of transpiration saves water, which is eventually released to ground water or streams. Hibbert (1967) summarized worldwide catchment studies and observed that deforestation increased and afforestation decreased water yield. He concluded that the response was highly variable and, for the most part, unpredictable. This might be expected when examining data from the great range of climates, vegetations, and geomorphic conditions which exist worldwide. Lull and Reinliart (1967) reviewed available information from forested catchment studies in the Northeastern United States looking for effects of partial or complete forest removal on yield response. They concluded that first year water yield increases were not highly sensitive to precipitation amount, that partial cuttings were not as efficient for augmenting water yield as were complete cuttings, and that water yield from well-stocked northeastern forests could be increased by from 4 to 12 inches the first year after complete cutting.

So, it is possible to remove trees to increase runoff, however the question is regarding sensibility. Do I feel like it is a reasonable ecosystem service loss of forest versus increases streamflow yield? My initial reaction is, no. Upon, further literature review regarding experiments at the Hubbard Brook experimental forest by Hornbeck and Kochenderfer(2001), its seems that it is possible to increase runoff through deforestation, but you run into a number of concerns:

  1. Increased timing of spring runoff. Peak flow could arrive earlier in the season, resulting in low streamflows by the late summer, when water yields may be essential for certain wildlife species.

  2. Nutrient cycling may change drastically, with potentially high increases in streamwater concentrations of nitrogen (as nitrate), hydrogen, and base cations. This nutrient increase may result in eutrophication to downstream lakes.

  3. Changes in forest type could occur after major deforestation within a region. Firstly, to maintain the increased water yield from deforesting a region, one must continually apply herbicide, so regrowth does not take back those water yield gains. This means a continual effort must be made to deforest a area every few years. Secondly, if regrowth is unmanaged, then the returning vegetation may be drastically different than what was previously there, impacting the habitat suitability for local wildlife.

For these reasons, I personally feel that it is not sensible to risk the potential adverse effects from deforestation for a short gain in water yield.
